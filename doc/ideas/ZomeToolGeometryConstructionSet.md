# 1. What “dual” actually does

Your intuition is essentially correct, but there are two different cases.

For the **surface of a polyhedron**, which is a two-dimensional cell complex,

[
\text{vertex}\longleftrightarrow\text{face},
\qquad
\text{edge}\longleftrightarrow\text{edge}.
]

Thus:

[
\text{cube}\longleftrightarrow\text{octahedron},
\qquad
\text{dodecahedron}\longleftrightarrow\text{icosahedron}.
]

For a genuine **three-dimensional tiling of space**, duality acts as

[
\begin{array}{c|c}
\text{primal object} & \text{dual object}\
\hline
\text{vertex} & \text{volume}\
\text{edge} & \text{polygonal face}\
\text{face} & \text{edge}\
\text{volume} & \text{vertex}.
\end{array}
]

So yes: a network of vertices and edges may be related to a system of volumes and faces.

But there is an important qualification:

> A bare graph does not contain enough information to determine a three-dimensional dual tiling.

The graph tells you which vertices are connected, but not:

* which cycles form faces;
* which faces enclose cells;
* the ordering of faces around an edge;
* whether a geometrically convex realization exists;
* whether the resulting dual faces can all be planar.

You need an **embedded cell complex**, not merely a graph.

There are three common geometric duals:

1. **Combinatorial or Poincaré dual**
   Defined from incidence relations. It always makes combinatorial sense for a suitable cell decomposition, but the straight-edged geometric realization may be ugly or nonconvex.

2. **Barycentric dual**
   Put dual vertices at cell centroids and connect them through face and edge centroids. It is robust, but produces many irregular pieces.

3. **Circumcentric dual: Delaunay–Voronoi**
   Delaunay tetrahedra are dual to Voronoi polyhedra. This produces meaningful convex cells, but generally not a small set of congruent or highly regular building blocks.

Therefore, directly dualizing an arbitrary Zometool construction would usually produce irregular volumes. It would not preserve the beautiful “few exact elements” property you want.

# 2. The deeper construction: projected cubes and zonotopes

The better idea is to start from several fixed direction vectors

[
\mathbf g_1,\ldots,\mathbf g_m
]

and interpret them as generators.

A **zonotope** is a Minkowski sum of line segments:

[
Z=\sum_{i=1}^{m}[0,\mathbf g_i]
===============================

\left{
\sum_i t_i\mathbf g_i; \middle|; 0\leq t_i\leq1
\right}.
]

This creates an immediate dimensional hierarchy:

[
\begin{aligned}
{\mathbf g_i}
&\rightarrow \text{edges},\
{\mathbf g_i,\mathbf g_j}
&\rightarrow \text{parallelograms or rhombi},\
{\mathbf g_i,\mathbf g_j,\mathbf g_k}
&\rightarrow \text{parallelepipeds or rhombohedra}.
\end{aligned}
]

In other words, a strut is not something fundamentally different from a polyhedral tile. It is merely a lower-dimensional cell generated by a subset of the same directions.

This is the volume analogue of Zometool.

## The six icosahedral generators

Let

[
\varphi=\frac{1+\sqrt5}{2}
]

and choose six vectors pointing along six opposite-vertex axes of an icosahedron, for example

[
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf g_1&=(1,\varphi,0),&
\mathbf g_2&=(-1,\varphi,0),\
\mathbf g_3&=(0,1,\varphi),&
\mathbf g_4&=(0,-1,\varphi),\
\mathbf g_5&=(\varphi,0,1),&
\mathbf g_6&=(\varphi,0,-1).
\end{aligned}
]

They all have the same length. After normalization, every pair has

[
\widehat{\mathbf g}_i\cdot\widehat{\mathbf g}_j
===============================================

\pm\frac{1}{\sqrt5}.
]

Consequently, the parallelogram generated by any pair is a **golden rhombus**. Its two diagonal lengths have ratio

[
\frac{d_{\rm long}}{d_{\rm short}}
==================================

\sqrt{
\frac{1+1/\sqrt5}
{1-1/\sqrt5}
}
=

\varphi.
]

This is not a decorative coincidence. The golden ratio follows directly from the icosahedral direction system.

Now select any three generators:

[
R_{ijk}
=======

\left{
t_i\mathbf g_i+t_j\mathbf g_j+t_k\mathbf g_k
; \middle|;
0\leq t_i,t_j,t_k\leq1
\right}.
]

This is a golden rhombohedron.

There are

[
\binom{6}{3}=20
]

triples, but under icosahedral symmetry they fall into only **two geometric classes**:

* ten prolate, or “thick”, golden rhombohedra;
* ten oblate, or “thin”, golden rhombohedra.

Their volumes differ by a factor of (\varphi). These are the two fundamental tiles of the Ammann–Kramer–Neri three-dimensional icosahedral tiling. The simplest icosahedral quasiperiodic space tilings indeed use these two rhombohedral prototiles. ([journals.iucr.org][1])

That is probably the cleanest answer to your requirement:

> **Six directions, one golden-rhombus face shape, two rhombohedral volume elements, yet arbitrarily complex three-dimensional structures.**

# 3. Why this is related to Zometool at a deeper level

The unifying parent object is the six-dimensional integer lattice

[
\mathbb Z^6.
]

Give each integer lattice point

[
\mathbf n=(n_1,\ldots,n_6)\in\mathbb Z^6
]

a projected physical position

[
\mathbf r_\parallel(\mathbf n)
==============================

\sum_{i=1}^{6}n_i\mathbf g_i.
]

Then the different cells of the six-dimensional cubic lattice project as follows:

[
\begin{array}{c|c}
\text{cell in }\mathbb Z^6 & \text{projection into 3D}\
\hline
0\text{-cube} & \text{vertex}\
1\text{-cube} & \text{Zome-like strut}\
2\text{-cube} & \text{golden rhombus}\
3\text{-cube} & \text{golden rhombohedron}.
\end{array}
]

Thus the graph system and the volume system are not primarily duals. They are the **1-skeleton and 3-cells of the same projected cubical complex**.

This is mathematically more natural than:

[
\text{graph}\rightarrow\text{dual volumes}.
]

The hierarchy is instead

[
\mathbb Z^6
\longrightarrow
\begin{cases}
\text{projected vertices},\
\text{projected edges},\
\text{projected faces},\
\text{projected volumes}.
\end{cases}
]

Three-dimensional icosahedral quasicrystal tilings can be generated by projecting suitable parts of a six-dimensional cubic lattice into three-dimensional physical space. ([math.uni-bielefeld.de][2])

The rhombic triacontahedron also appears naturally here: it is a six-generator zonohedron and can be obtained as an icosahedral projection of a six-dimensional cube. ([Wikimedia Commons][3])

# 4. Where aperiodicity enters

Simply allowing the two rhombohedra to connect along matching faces is **not sufficient** to force a quasiperiodic structure.

Without additional restrictions, the same shapes can form:

* periodic tilings;
* quasiperiodic tilings;
* locally valid but globally blocked patches;
* structures containing phason defects;
* arbitrary disordered rhombohedral packings.

You need one of three global-order mechanisms.

## A. Cut-and-project rule

Split six-dimensional space into

[
\mathbb R^6
===========

E_\parallel\oplus E_\perp,
]

where both (E_\parallel) and (E_\perp) are three-dimensional.

For every lattice point (\mathbf n),

[
\mathbf r_\parallel=P_\parallel\mathbf n,
\qquad
\mathbf r_\perp=P_\perp\mathbf n.
]

Keep the point only when

[
\mathbf r_\perp\in W,
]

where (W) is an acceptance window in perpendicular space, often related to a rhombic triacontahedron.

Physical space tells you **where the element is**. Perpendicular space tells you **whether it is allowed**.

This is the most elegant global generator because the apparently complicated three-dimensional structure is merely a regular six-dimensional lattice viewed through an irrational projection.

## B. Matching rules

Decorate faces or edges with:

* arrows;
* colors;
* line segments;
* parity marks;
* hierarchical labels.

Only matching decorations may touch.

Ammann-type facet decorations can enforce the intended structure much as matching arrows do in Penrose tilings. The mathematical literature explicitly treats decorated facets of the two Ammann rhombohedra. ([Semantic Scholar][4])

This is best for an interactive construction game because every placement can be checked locally.

The complication is that a simple local rule may not immediately reveal whether a partially built finite patch can be extended indefinitely.

## C. Inflation or substitution

Replace every large tile by a fixed patch of smaller tiles, or inversely merge legal patches into supertiles.

The scale factor is usually a power of (\varphi). This exposes the hierarchical nature of the structure:

[
\text{tile}
\rightarrow
\text{supertile}
\rightarrow
\text{super-supertile}
\rightarrow\cdots
]

This would make a very compelling construction mechanic: the user builds locally, while the engine detects larger emergent legal structures.

# 5. A practical engine architecture

I would store the structure in six-dimensional integer coordinates and use three-dimensional floating-point coordinates only for rendering.

## Exact vertex representation

```text
Vertex:
    int n[6]
```

Its rendered position is

[
\mathbf r=P_\parallel\mathbf n.
]

This solves several problems immediately:

* exact vertex equality;
* no accumulated floating-point drift;
* exact hashing;
* exact connectivity;
* easy translation;
* exact identification of rhombus and rhombohedron orientations.

Although rendered coordinates contain (\varphi), topological identity remains purely integer.

## Tile representation

A rhombohedron can be represented as

```text
Tile:
    int base[6]
    uint8 i, j, k
```

where (i,j,k) identify the three generator directions.

Its eight vertices are

[
\mathbf n_{\epsilon_i\epsilon_j\epsilon_k}
==========================================

\mathbf n_0+
\epsilon_i\mathbf e_i+
\epsilon_j\mathbf e_j+
\epsilon_k\mathbf e_k,
\qquad
\epsilon_i,\epsilon_j,\epsilon_k\in{0,1}.
]

You do not need to store eight arbitrary 3D points.

## Face representation

A face is determined by:

* two generator indices (i,j);
* a six-dimensional anchor;
* which side of the third generator it lies on.

Canonicalize the four integer vertex identifiers and use them as a hash-map key:

```text
face_key = sorted(v0, v1, v2, v3)
```

An exposed face has one incident tile. A closed interior face has two.

Therefore attachment is exact and essentially (O(1)).

## Candidate attachment

When the user selects a free rhombic face generated by (i,j):

1. enumerate possible third directions (k\neq i,j);
2. construct the tile on the outward side;
3. reject overlap;
4. check matching labels;
5. optionally check perpendicular-space acceptance;
6. insert its six faces into the face hash table.

Because there are only six directions, the candidate count is tiny.

# 6. Several useful game or engine modes

The same core engine could expose different mathematical regimes.

### Free construction mode

Only geometry and non-overlap matter.

This is the volume equivalent of ordinary Zometool: highly symmetric objects, shells, stars, clusters and unusual periodic structures can all be built.

### Quasicrystal mode

A hidden perpendicular-space coordinate is maintained. Only placements consistent with a chosen acceptance window are allowed.

The user experiences local building, but every legal object belongs to a globally coherent cut-and-project structure.

### Matching-rule mode

The player sees face decorations and must match them.

This is more comprehensible as a puzzle and allows the mathematics to be discovered visually.

### Defect mode

Allow violations but assign them a cost:

[
E=
\lambda_{\rm overlap}N_{\rm overlap}
+
\lambda_{\rm match}N_{\rm mismatch}
+
\lambda_\perp E_{\rm phason}.
]

Such violations can be interpreted as:

* phason defects;
* dislocations;
* grain boundaries;
* incompatible local patches.

That could make the construction system considerably richer than a pure mathematical demonstrator.

### Approximant mode

Replace the irrational projection by rational approximations to (\varphi), using successive Fibonacci ratios:

[
\varphi\approx
\frac{F_{n+1}}{F_n}.
]

This produces large periodic approximants that become increasingly quasicrystalline. It gives a continuous conceptual bridge:

[
\text{small periodic cell}
\rightarrow
\text{large approximant}
\rightarrow
\text{ideal quasicrystal}.
]

# 7. The dual structure is still very useful

After building the rhombohedral cell complex, generate its dual:

* one dual vertex at each rhombohedron center;
* one dual edge through each shared rhombic face;
* one dual polygon around each primal edge;
* one dual polyhedral cell around each primal vertex.

So you obtain two coupled representations:

[
\boxed{\text{rhombohedral material}}
\qquad\longleftrightarrow\qquad
\boxed{\text{Zome-like skeletal network}}.
]

These can have different interpretations:

| Primal rhombohedral complex | Dual network          |
| --------------------------- | --------------------- |
| solid material              | frame or truss        |
| tile                        | junction              |
| shared face                 | strut                 |
| primal vertex               | enclosed dual chamber |
| tile decoration             | network state         |

This could be an excellent engine feature: switch interactively between **solid**, **skeleton** and **dual-cell** views.

But the dual cells will generally have several different and less regular shapes. Therefore, I would make the rhombohedra the fundamental construction elements and the dual system a derived representation.

# 8. A more elaborate alternative: canonical-cell tilings

There is another system worth considering after the two-rhombohedron engine works: the **canonical-cell tiling** used for modelling icosahedral quasicrystals.

It uses four basic polyhedral cells rather than two. These cells describe networks of icosahedral atomic clusters and related periodic approximants. ([Wiley Online Library][5])

This has advantages:

* richer local environments;
* more interesting cavities and cluster connectivity;
* closer relation to real quasicrystal structures;
* better suitability for architecture-like assemblies.

But it is less minimal and less immediately transparent than the golden-rhombohedron system.

My ordering would be:

1. **Two golden rhombohedra** as the fundamental engine.
2. Dual graph and dual cell visualization.
3. Inflation and cut-and-project modes.
4. Canonical four-cell system as an advanced tileset.
5. User-defined generator stars and arbitrary zonotopal systems.

# 9. The deepest abstraction: oriented matroids

There is an even more abstract mathematical level beneath this.

For every triple of generators, define

[
\chi(i,j,k)
===========

\operatorname{sign}
\det(\mathbf g_i,\mathbf g_j,\mathbf g_k).
]

The collection of these signs is the **chirotope** of a rank-three oriented matroid.

It captures the combinatorial geometry:

* which triples have positive or negative orientation;
* which generators are coplanar;
* how cells can meet;
* how faces are ordered;
* which combinatorial structures remain unchanged under continuous deformation.

This separates four layers beautifully:

[
\begin{array}{c|c}
\text{layer} & \text{mathematical object}\
\hline
\text{metric geometry} & \mathbf g_i,\ \varphi,\ \text{lengths and angles}\
\text{combinatorics} & \text{oriented matroid}\
\text{topology} & \text{cell complex and its dual}\
\text{global order} & \text{cut-and-project window or substitution}.
\end{array}
]

A zonotope itself is an affine projection of a higher-dimensional cube, and oriented-matroid duality is one of the natural tools for understanding zonotopal space tilings. ([arXiv][6])

This is likely the “mathematically elegant and deep” foundation you are looking for. It is not merely a collection of attractive polyhedra. It connects:

* Coxeter and icosahedral symmetry;
* algebraic number fields (\mathbb Q(\sqrt5));
* higher-dimensional lattices;
* projection geometry;
* aperiodic order;
* substitution systems;
* cellular complexes;
* duality;
* oriented matroids;
* exact computational geometry.

# 1. The fundamental object is an oriented interface

A slot should not be represented only by a direction vector (\mathbf d).

It needs a complete right-handed frame

[
F=(\mathbf x,\mathbf y,\mathbf z),
]

where:

* (\mathbf z) is the outward direction of the slot;
* (\mathbf x,\mathbf y) define the rotational orientation of the keyed hole;
* the origin is the physical mating point.

For example, two square slots may point in the same direction but differ by (45^\circ). They are not geometrically compatible.

In the code this is:

```cpp
struct Pose {
    Mat3 R;   // columns are x, y, z
    Vec3 p;   // interface origin
};

struct SlotDef {
    ConnectorId connector;
    SlotKey key;
    Pose local;
    SlotId opposite;
    uint16_t orbit;
    uint16_t matchTag;
    int8_t polarity;
};
```

The slot pose is stored in the local frame of the node type.

The world-space slot frame is therefore

[
F_{\mathrm{slot}}^{\mathrm{world}}
==================================

T_{\mathrm{node}}F_{\mathrm{slot}}^{\mathrm{local}}.
]

This is the single most important geometric operation in the system.

# 2. Distinguish two kinds of rotational symmetry

There are two related but conceptually distinct numbers.

## Slot-direction stabilizer

This tells you how much rotational symmetry the **node geometry** has around the slot direction.

For an icosahedral node:

* vertex axes are 5-fold;
* face-normal axes are 3-fold;
* edge-midpoint axes are 2-fold.

## Connector-profile symmetry

This tells you how many rotations leave the **physical plug cross-section** unchanged:

| Cross-section  | Profile fold |
| -------------- | -----------: |
| asymmetric key |            1 |
| rectangle      |            2 |
| triangle       |            3 |
| square         |            4 |
| pentagon       |            5 |
| hexagon        |            6 |
| circular       |   continuous |

These often coincide in Zometool, but your generalized engine should not assume that they always do.

The standard Zometool system uses rectangular blue, triangular yellow, and pentagonal red interfaces. The corresponding directions lie on the 2-fold, 3-fold and 5-fold axes of the node. ([georgehart.com][1])

In the starter:

```cpp
struct ConnectorType {
    uint8_t fold;
    uint16_t family;
    bool chiral;
};
```

The allowed phase is quantized as

[
\theta_k=\frac{2\pi k}{n},
\qquad k=0,\ldots,n-1.
]

A future circular connector could use a special `fold == 0` convention and allow continuous twist.

# 3. How two interfaces match

I use the convention that local (+\mathbf z) points **outward from the owning rigid body**.

Two interfaces match when:

1. their origins coincide;
2. their outward directions oppose one another;
3. their connector families are compatible;
4. their in-plane frames differ by an allowed discrete phase;
5. optional matching tags and polarities agree.

Geometrically:

[
\mathbf p_A=\mathbf p_B,
]

[
\mathbf z_A=-\mathbf z_B,
]

and, for an (n)-fold connector,

[
F_B
===

F_A R_z\left(\frac{2\pi k}{n}\right)R_x(\pi).
]

The (R_x(\pi)) reverses the interface direction while maintaining a right-handed frame.

This is implemented by:

```cpp
inline Pose mateOf(const Pose& A, double phase) {
    const Mat3 flipX{
        {1,0,0},
        {0,-1,0},
        {0,0,-1}
    };

    return A * Pose{
        rotZ(phase) * flipX,
        {0,0,0}
    };
}
```

This formulation works for:

* straight struts;
* bent struts;
* polyhedral rigid elements;
* different connectors at opposite ends;
* keyed orientation;
* male/female connectors;
* Ammann arrows or color matching.

A strut is simply a rigid body with two interface frames:

```cpp
struct StrutEnd {
    ConnectorId connector;
    Pose local;
    uint16_t matchTag;
    int8_t polarity;
};

struct StrutType {
    double length;
    StrutEnd end[2];
};
```

A curved or kinked strut requires no new mathematics. Its two end frames simply have a more complicated relative transform.

# 4. Ideal slot numbering

Do not make the semantic meaning of a slot depend solely on its position in an array.

Use two identities:

```cpp
using SlotId = uint16_t;   // dense array index

struct SlotKey {
    uint16_t family;
    uint16_t axis;
    int8_t sign;
};
```

`SlotId` is optimized for memory access.

`SlotKey` expresses the mathematics:

[
\text{slot}
===========

(\text{family},\text{unoriented axis},\text{sign}).
]

For a Zometool-like node, I would use:

| Family         | Axes | Oriented slots | Dense range |
| -------------- | ---: | -------------: | ----------: |
| red, 5-fold    |    6 |             12 |        0–11 |
| yellow, 3-fold |   10 |             20 |       12–31 |
| blue, 2-fold   |   15 |             30 |       32–61 |

Thus:

```cpp
slot = familyBase + 2*axis + signBit;
```

where:

```cpp
signBit = 0; // positive direction
signBit = 1; // negative direction
```

The 62 slots correspond to opposite pairs associated with the six vertex axes, ten face axes and fifteen edge axes of the icosahedral structure. ([St. Bonaventure University][2])

This gives the useful property:

```cpp
slot.opposite
```

without searching geometrically.

I would still store `opposite` explicitly because it makes the runtime code faster and protects you from assumptions about numbering in custom node types.

# 5. Immutable types versus mutable instances

The geometric description of a node is shared by all instances.

```cpp
struct NodeType {
    double radius;
    std::vector<SlotDef> slots;
    std::vector<SymmetryOp> symmetry;
};
```

A node instance stores only:

```cpp
struct NodeInstance {
    NodeTypeId type;
    Pose world;
    std::vector<EdgeId> edgeAtSlot;
};
```

Similarly, a strut type stores its shape and interface geometry, while an edge instance stores connectivity:

```cpp
struct EdgeInstance {
    NodeId node[2];
    SlotId slot[2];
    StrutTypeId type;

    uint16_t phaseStep[2];

    Pose world;
};
```

The resulting graph is therefore not merely:

```text
node A -------- node B
```

but:

```text
node A, slot 17
        |
        | strut type 3
        | end phase 1
        |
node B, slot 42
```

This is enough to reconstruct the complete rigid geometry.

# 6. The fundamental editing operation: grow from a slot

The operation is specified by:

```cpp
struct GrowRequest {
    NodeId sourceNode;
    SlotId sourceSlot;
    StrutTypeId strutType;

    NodeTypeId targetNodeType;
    SlotId targetSlot;

    uint16_t sourcePhaseStep;
    uint16_t targetPhaseStep;
};
```

The algorithm is:

1. Obtain the source slot in world coordinates.
2. Mate strut end 0 to it.
3. Compute the strut world transform.
4. Transform strut end 1 into world coordinates.
5. Mate the selected target-node slot to strut end 1.
6. Compute the target node pose.
7. Validate the entire operation.
8. Commit the new node and strut.

In equations:

[
F_A=T_A S_A,
]

[
T_{\mathrm{strut}}
==================

\operatorname{Mate}(F_A,\theta_A)
E_0^{-1},
]

[
F_1=T_{\mathrm{strut}}E_1,
]

[
T_B
===

\operatorname{Mate}(F_1,\theta_B)
S_B^{-1}.
]

Here:

* (S_A,S_B) are node-local slot transforms;
* (E_0,E_1) are strut-local end transforms;
* (T_A,T_B) are node world transforms.

The code exposes:

```cpp
GrowCandidate previewGrow(const GrowRequest& q) const;
std::pair<NodeId,EdgeId> commitGrow(const GrowCandidate& c);
std::pair<NodeId,EdgeId> grow(const GrowRequest& q);
```

Separating `preview` and `commit` is important. It lets the editor display the proposed part in transparent form before insertion and lets a symmetric edit validate all copies before modifying anything.

# 7. Closing loops

Suppose the user grows several struts and the final strut should return to an already existing node.

You should not create a duplicate node and later try to merge it.

Instead:

1. Predict the required target-node pose.
2. Search for an existing node near that pose.
3. Verify type, orientation and free slot.
4. connect to it directly.

The starter implements:

```cpp
EdgeId connectExisting(
    const GrowRequest& request,
    NodeId existingTarget,
    double positionTolerance,
    double rotationTolerance
);
```

This is the primitive needed to close:

* triangles;
* rectangles;
* pentagons;
* polyhedral faces;
* larger periodic cycles.

For mathematically exact systems, this tolerance comparison should later be replaced by exact coordinate identity.

# 8. Symmetry operations should be slot permutations

A symmetry operation should not be applied by repeatedly rotating floating-point vectors and searching approximately.

Precompute how each symmetry operation acts on the finite slot set:

```cpp
struct SlotMap {
    SlotId slot;
    int16_t phaseStep;
};

struct SymmetryOp {
    Mat3 localRotation;
    std::vector<SlotMap> map;
};
```

For each original slot (i), the table says:

[
g(i)=j,
]

and

[
gF_i
====

F_j R_z(\Delta\theta_{g,i}).
]

The `phaseStep` is necessary because a symmetry operation may map the slot direction correctly but rotate its keyed frame around that direction.

The starter constructs this table automatically:

```cpp
SymmetryOp makeSymmetryOp(
    const NodeType& node,
    const std::vector<ConnectorType>& connectors,
    const Mat3& rotation
);
```

Then a fourfold appendage is:

```cpp
std::vector<uint16_t> C4{0,1,2,3};

construction.growOrbit(
    centerNode,
    seedSlot,
    C4,
    strutType,
    targetNodeType,
    targetSlot
);
```

The implementation:

1. computes the orbit of the seed slot;
2. eliminates duplicates caused by stabilizers;
3. adjusts the keyed phase;
4. previews every appendage;
5. commits them only after all are valid.

This naturally supports:

[
C_n,\quad D_n,\quad T,\quad O,\quad I
]

and arbitrary user-defined finite symmetry groups.

For example:

* (C_5): five appendages around one axis;
* (D_5): five rotational copies plus reflection-related copies;
* tetrahedral group: four or twelve copies depending on the selected orbit;
* icosahedral group: orbits of size 12, 20, 30 or 60.

# 9. The graph is primary, but faces must be explicit

A closed graph cycle does not necessarily tell you that it is intended to be a face.

Therefore store faces explicitly as oriented edge cycles:

```cpp
struct OrientedEdge {
    EdgeId edge;
    bool reversed;
};

struct FaceInstance {
    uint16_t type;
    std::vector<OrientedEdge> boundary;
};
```

A face boundary is:

[
\partial f
==========

e_0+e_1+\cdots+e_{n-1}.
]

The orientation matters. Reversing the face reverses every edge and their order.

A face-building operation should work from a template such as:

```text
triangle:
    corner 0: enter slot a, leave slot b
    corner 1: enter slot a, leave slot b
    corner 2: enter slot a, leave slot b
```

or, more generally:

```cpp
struct FaceCornerTemplate {
    NodeTypeId nodeType;

    SlotId incomingSlot;
    SlotId outgoingSlot;

    StrutTypeId outgoingStrut;
    uint16_t outgoingPhase;
};
```

The algorithm to instantiate a face is:

```text
start from an existing node and oriented slot
for each template corner:
    use existing compatible edge if present
    otherwise preview a new strut and node
at the final corner:
    require exact closure onto the first node
validate planarity and non-self-intersection
commit all missing elements atomically
store the oriented edge cycle
```

For a perfectly predefined system, planarity should follow from the template. Checking it is still valuable for debugging.

# 10. Polyhedral cells require oriented faces

Similarly:

```cpp
struct OrientedFace {
    uint32_t face;
    bool reversed;
};

struct CellInstance {
    uint16_t type;
    std::vector<OrientedFace> boundary;
};
```

A valid closed cell should satisfy:

[
\partial(\partial C)=0.
]

Computationally, that means every boundary edge appears exactly twice with opposite orientation.

This is an excellent and simple validation rule:

```text
for each oriented face of cell:
    for each oriented boundary edge:
        edgeBalance[edge] += orientationSign

cell is closed iff edgeBalance[edge] == 0 for every edge
```

For a tiling or cellular complex, also require:

```text
one face may belong to:
    1 cell  -> exposed boundary
    2 cells -> internal face
    >2      -> non-manifold error
```

Thus the dimensional hierarchy becomes:

```text
NodeInstance
    owns occupied slots

EdgeInstance
    connects two node slots

FaceInstance
    owns an oriented cycle of edges

CellInstance
    owns an oriented shell of faces
```

# 11. Matching rules for aperiodic structures

Connector geometry answers:

> Can these two pieces physically connect?

Aperiodic matching labels answer:

> Is this connection allowed by the tiling grammar?

That is why `SlotDef` and `StrutEnd` contain:

```cpp
uint16_t matchTag;
int8_t polarity;
```

You could interpret them as:

```text
matchTag:
    color
    Ammann line type
    arrow type
    hierarchical level
    local-state identifier

polarity:
    -1: inward / female / arrow tail
     0: neutral
    +1: outward / male / arrow head
```

A simple compatibility rule is:

```cpp
same connector family
AND compatible cross-section phase
AND equal nonzero matchTag
AND opposite nonzero polarity
```

More sophisticated rules can use:

```cpp
bool compatible(
    const InterfaceSignature& a,
    const InterfaceSignature& b,
    int relativePhase
);
```

This keeps the editing engine general. Penrose-like arrows, Wang-tile colors, molecular valence rules and mechanical keys all become variations of the same interface grammar.

# 12. Exact representation versus floating-point representation

The current starter uses rigid `Pose` transforms with doubles. This is appropriate for:

* prototyping;
* rendering;
* arbitrary user-defined pieces;
* non-periodic free construction.

But a perfectly mathematical Zometool or quasicrystal engine should eventually have an exact layer.

For an icosahedral system, positions can be stored as:

[
\mathbf n\in\mathbb Z^6
]

or as coordinates in

[
\mathbb Z[\varphi]^3.
]

A compact exact node could look like:

```cpp
struct Lattice6 {
    int32_t n[6];
};

struct ExactNodeInstance {
    Lattice6 position;
    NodeTypeId type;

    uint8_t orientationGroupElement;
    uint64_t occupiedSlots;
};
```

The `orientationGroupElement` is an index into a finite rotation group:

```cpp
Mat3 groupRotation[60];
```

rather than an arbitrary quaternion.

Then:

* node equality is exact;
* loop closure is exact;
* hashing is exact;
* no floating-point drift accumulates;
* rotations act by integer-coordinate transformations or precomputed tables.

World coordinates are derived only for rendering:

[
\mathbf r=P_\parallel\mathbf n.
]

My recommended final architecture is therefore:

```text
Exact combinatorial layer
    integer lattice position
    finite orientation-group index
    slot IDs
    edge/face/cell incidence

Derived geometric layer
    Vec3 positions
    Mat3 or quaternion orientations
    meshes
    collision geometry
```

The supplied starter implements the geometric layer first because it is much easier to experiment with, but its slot and symmetry-table design transfers directly to the exact version.

# 13. Memory layout considerations

The starter uses:

```cpp
std::vector<EdgeId> edgeAtSlot;
```

for every node. This is clear and gives (O(1)) slot occupancy lookup.

For a 62-slot node, however, that is approximately:

[
62\times4=248\ \text{bytes per node}
]

only for the edge table.

For a large sparse construction, most slots will be empty. A more efficient representation is:

```cpp
struct CompactNode {
    NodeTypeId type;
    OrientationId orientation;
    Lattice6 position;

    uint64_t occupiedMask;

    uint32_t incidentBegin;
    uint16_t incidentCount;
};
```

with a global array:

```cpp
struct IncidentEdge {
    SlotId slot;
    EdgeId edge;
};
```

Because a Zometool-like node has 62 slots, the complete occupancy state fits almost perfectly into one `uint64_t`.

Then:

```cpp
occupied = node.occupiedMask & (uint64_t(1) << slot);
```

Finding the corresponding edge requires searching only the node’s small incident list. If average valence is perhaps 3–10, this is much smaller than storing 62 edge IDs per node.

I would use:

* the simple dense table in the editor prototype;
* occupancy mask plus compact incidence lists for large constructions;
* structure-of-arrays only when profiling shows a need.

# 14. The validation pipeline

Every editing operation should be a transaction:

```text
propose
validate
commit
```

Validation should proceed from cheapest to most expensive:

1. source slot exists;
2. source slot is free;
3. connector families match;
4. keyed phases are allowed;
5. matching tags and polarities agree;
6. resulting node does not duplicate an incompatible existing node;
7. exact or tolerance-based closure is satisfied;
8. strut does not duplicate an existing edge;
9. broad-phase collision check;
10. precise collision check;
11. face manifold constraints;
12. cell manifold constraints;
13. optional aperiodic acceptance-window rule.

Symmetric edits should preview **all copies first**. Either all copies are committed, or none are.

That atomicity prevents a failed icosahedral operation from leaving eleven successful appendages and one missing appendage.
